Dance, Anatomy, and Social Media

An exploration of how dance-focused Instagrams do — or do not — promote healthy dance practices as understood through anatomy.

Social Media & Dance

Regardless of your opinions on the rise of social media in the 21st century, it‚Äôs near impossible to argue against the reality that social media platforms have had a profound influence on the ways that people share and consume information. In many ways, this has significant benefits to dancers in training &emdash; the social media accounts of professional dancers as well as ‚Äúcelebrity‚Äù students can provide inspiration and motivation by offering a glimpse into their day-to-day lives.

Social media posts tend to be carefully crafted to portray certain themes and send implicit messages. Instagram, in particular, is often accused of promoting an excessively ‚Äúimage-focused‚Äù online presence. There are many popular dance accounts on Instagram, spanning across different styles, levels of experience, and geographic locations.




Dance & Anatomy

As an art from, dance tends to prioritize aesthetic over all else. Some valued qualities in dancers are not anatomically "natural", meaning that dancers must train to achieve them. While dance accounts on Instagram can offer inspiration and meaningful advice, they can also implicitly encourage unsafe practices for dance training.

In this exploration, we will focus on three topics: extended static stretching, hip impingement (related to training for extensions), and foot stretching. Since these are all common topics on Instagram, we will only consider a small sampling of posts related to these areas.

Static Stretching

If you're an Instagram user, you've probably seen the pictures — the professional dancer sprawled in oversplit, casually messing with their phone or eating a snack. Despite this popular image, extended static stretching is not an effective practice for improving functional flexibility.

Training for Hip Extension

Sky-high extensions are a popular aesthetic in dance. Tips for achieving these extensions are certainly a common topic on Instagram; however, some suggestions are potentially harmful for some dancers, leading to joint issues.

Foot Stretching

Another quasi-obsession in dance (particularly in ballet) is the highly arched, excessively flexible foot. Popular dance accounts often post about foot-stretching — the practice of using various devices to increase static foot flexibility.

Long Static Stretching

Extreme flexibility has become a norm in the dance world, especially on competitive dance circuits. It's not uncommon to see dancers stretching far past a 180° split. Working to achieve this level of flexibility is a common topic on Instagram. Unfortunately, some unsafe stretching practices are often portrayed. One of these is the idea of "sitting" in splits &emdash; a practice in which dancers will hold their splits or oversplits for minutes at a time in an attempt to increase their flexibility.

Sitting in splits for an extended period of time can have a variety of negative effects on the dancers. First, according to physical therapist Marika Molnar, holding static stretches for longer than 30 seconds will loosen ligaments (more potential for injury), as well as injure cartilage in your hip. In an oversplit, the head of the femur can press into the acetabulum at a damaging angle, sometimes leading to labral tears. Dr. William Hamilton adds that extreme static stretching can cause joint destabilization.

In addition to these issues, holding static stretches for longer than 20 or 30 seconds actually reduces muscular performance. This means that dancers who do significant amounts of static stretching, especially prior to dancing, will have a lesser ability to jump and lift their legs &emdash; which is the direct opposite effect of what a dancer wants.

Femoroacetabular Impingement (FAI), also commonly called hip impingement, is a condition the causes pain in the hip due to repetitive contact between the femur and the pelvis at the acetabulum during end-range motion. Because dancers tend to have a large range of motion in this joint, often coupled with instability due to extreme stretching, hip impingement is a relatively common condition in dancers.

Unfortunately, some common stretching and strengthening practice in dance training can increase the risk of hip impingement. One of these is the use of extreme force when stretching the hips &emdash; such as using weights to pull legs downwards in middle splits.

When doing a grand battement, a dancer with an extreme range of motion is already at risk for causing bony contact or damaging soft tissue. The addition of the weights on the ankles worsens this problem. When the femoral head makes contact with the acetabulum (either anterior or posterior to the center), the point of contact can act as a fulcrum for the femoral head to begin to slide out of the acetabulum in the direction opposite of the contact. For instance, a dancer doing a grand battement to the front could have contact between the anterior surface of the femoral head and the acetabulum, causing the femoral head to slide slightly backwards.

When a dancer has an ankle weight on their ankle and does a high kick, the leg essentially becomes a weighted lever arm, which increases the force acting at the fulcrum of bony contact in the hip.

The use of these training techniques over time can have a negative effect on a dancer's long-term hip joint health. While some bony remodelling of the hip may be desirable in dancers aiming for professional careers, permanent damage can also be caused.

Foot Stretching

Back to Menu

The aesthetic of the highly arched foot is valued in many dance forms, but particularly in ballet. A dancer‚Äôs range of motion in their foot and ankle, however, is often determined more by joint mobility than the flexibility of any muscle. Many dancers not born with naturally arched feet turn to various methods to increase their range of motion. While exercises to strengthen the foot and ankle and improve control are common, so is another method ‚Äì foot stretching. Dancers force their foot and ankle into an arch past their comfortable range of motion, holding this position for long periods of time.

Foot stretching is a controversial practice in dance. The relative safety of foot-stretching is much debated, and many teachers make the argument that even if foot-stretching is not entirely healthy, it's a necessary method to achieve desirable lines. One thing's for certain though &emdash; Instagram loves foot stretchers.

Foot stretching posts, often using devices specifically built for this purpose, pop up everywhere. A few examples are below.

From an anatomical persepective, foot stretching can have limited benefits, but it also carries risk for injury. Foot stretchers primarily stretch connective tissue in the foot and ankle rather than muscle, and they don't strengthen the foot or ankle. Stretching ligaments in the foot can cause instability in many foot joints, as well as lower a dancer's proprioception. Together, this increases the chance of injury while dancing.

Boyd Bender, a physical therapist for Pacific Northwest Ballet, also points out that foot stretchers will generally only be effective until a dancer reaches skeletal maturity. The reason for this is that the biggest limiting factor in developing a pretty point is joint mobility, which cannot be significantly changed by stretching soft tissues.

While there are valid argument for why foot stretchers should not be used, or only used with caution, some people in the dance world are strong proponents of them. Obviously, the manufacturers of foot stretchers argue that they are safe.

David Campos, the inventor of the Ballet Foot Stretch (a popular foot stretcher, seen in several of the Instagram posts above) claims that his design is safe compared to more "traditional" foot stretching methods, in which dancers stuff their feet underneath furniture or have peers force plantar flexion to stretch their feet.

Interestingly, at least one medical professional claims that user this specific foot stretcher is relatively safe. Dr. Nuria Masso, a sports medicine specialist, says that the way that the foot stretcher supports the back of the ankle helps to prevent tendinopathies and posterior impingement. The support against the downward force on the foot allows for space to be maintained between the tibia, calcaneus, and talus bones. In the image below, the arrow points to the space where impingment could occur when foot stretching.


In conclusion? Foot stretching probably isn't all good or all bad. It may be a necessary "evil" for dancers aiming for a professional career, but should likely only be used with caution and in conjunction with strengthening exercises.
